## Main Finding

The accounting object is strongest when it asks:

> What happens when a locally valid system is forced into a global/class closure?

The answer is not always collapse.

The possible transitions are:

- productive closure;
- terminal closure;
- captured closure;
- class exit;
- boundary import;
- obstruction relocation;
- absorbed obstruction;
- productive residue;
- source exhaustion;
- recursive feedback collapse.

This is more precise than "emergence versus collapse."

## Hypotheses To Carry Forward

### H1: Closure Transition Hypothesis

Collapse and emergence should first be classified by what kind of closure transition occurs: productive, terminal, captured, absorbed, or class-exiting.

### H2: Recursive Source Collapse Hypothesis

Systems collapse when they recursively consume their own outputs as source inputs while treating those outputs as independent source material.

### H3: Boundary Import Burden Hypothesis

Every claimed self-contained emergence must declare which external source supplies generation, admissibility, memory, legitimacy, intervention, or reconciliation.

### H4: Productive Obstruction Hypothesis

Some obstructions preserve emergence by preventing premature completion and forcing new admissibility or source growth.

### H5: No-Go/Escape Ledger Hypothesis

Class-relative no-go results compose only when their assumptions, escape costs, imported structures, and surviving residues are explicitly preserved.

### H6: Record-Source Separation Hypothesis

A completed record can preserve observer reconstruction while the source-generating process has collapsed.
